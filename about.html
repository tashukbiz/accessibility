About

I’m a digital accessibility specialist with a background in design and front-end development. I test the way real people do, and I write findings that teams can actually act on.

I’ve spent around a decade in UX/UI design and, before that, worked as an HTML developer building clean, semantic markup. That mix is the reason I moved into accessibility: I can look at a page as a designer, read it as a developer, and test it as someone who relies on the keyboard or a screen reader. When I find a problem, I usually know the pattern that fixes it.

What keeps me here is that the work is both technical and human. A government jobs site, a login, a booking form — for many people with a disability, one broken control is the difference between doing something themselves and not being able to at all. Fixing the code gives someone their independence back. That’s a good reason to get the details right.

I’m based in Sydney and work remotely across Australia. I hold permanent residency, and I’m working towards the IAAP CPACC certification.

What I work with

Standards & method

WCAG 2.2 Level AA success criteria; the W3C WCAG-EM evaluation method; the ARIA Authoring Practices Guide for correct patterns.

Testing

Keyboard-only testing; screen readers (VoiceOver on macOS and iOS, NVDA on Windows); CSS-off, zoom and text-spacing checks; automated tools (axe, WAVE, Lighthouse) used as a starting point, not the answer.

Design & front-end

Semantic HTML and ARIA; component-based design in Figma; UX writing and clear documentation for both technical and non-technical readers.

A note on automated tools and AI

Automated scanners are useful, but they only catch a portion of real accessibility problems — the rest need a person to test the actual experience. I use tools and AI to work faster, then rely on manual testing and judgement to check what they can’t: whether a keyboard user can finish the task, and whether a screen-reader user is told what they need to know.
